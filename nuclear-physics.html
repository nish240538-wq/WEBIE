Nuclear Physics – Maharashtra Board HSC & CBSE Class 12

The nucleus is the tiny, dense core of an atom containing protons and neutrons (nucleons). Discovered by Rutherford, the nucleus holds almost all the mass of the atom but occupies only 10−15 of its volume. This chapter is extremely important for Maharashtra HSC Board exams (Chapter 14: Atoms & Nuclei) and carries 6–8 marks in CBSE + heavy weightage in JEE Main & Advanced.

Mass Defect and Binding Energy

The actual mass of a nucleus is always less than the sum of masses of its individual protons and neutrons. This difference is called mass defect (Δm).

\[ \Delta m = Z m_p + (A - Z) m_n - M \] where \( Z \) = atomic number, \( A \) = mass number, \( m_p \) = mass of proton, \( m_n \) = mass of neutron, \( M \) = actual nuclear mass.

The missing mass is converted into binding energy that holds the nucleus together (Einstein’s mass-energy equivalence).

\[ BE = \Delta m \cdot c^2 = \Delta m \times 931.5 \, \text{MeV/u} \]

Binding energy per nucleon tells stability. The curve peaks at iron-56 (most stable nucleus). Lighter nuclei fuse, heavier nuclei fission to release energy.

Radioactivity – Alpha, Beta, Gamma Decay

Spontaneous disintegration of unstable nuclei. Three types of radiation:

  • α-decay: Helium nucleus emitted (mass ↓4, charge ↓2)
  • β-decay: Electron or positron emitted (mass same, charge ±1)
  • γ-decay: High-energy photon (no change in mass or charge)

Law of radioactive decay (first order):

\[ N = N_0 e^{-\lambda t} \quad \text{or} \quad \frac{dN}{dt} = -\lambda N \]

Half-life: \( T_{1/2} = \frac{0.693}{\lambda} \)
Mean life: \( \tau = \frac{1}{\lambda} \)

Cosmic Radiation & Natural Radioactivity

Earth is constantly bombarded by high-energy particles from space (cosmic rays). Astronauts in deep space face severe radiation exposure.

This explains why natural radioactivity exists on Earth and why shielding is critical in space missions.

JEE PYQ Analysis – How Nuclear Physics Appears in Competitive Exams

Every year JEE Main has 1–2 questions (8–12 marks) and JEE Advanced has 1 numerical or conceptual question from this chapter. Maharashtra HSC board repeats 4–6 mark numericals almost every year.

PYQ Type 1: Mass Defect & Binding Energy (Most Repeated)

Example (JEE Main 2024 pattern): Find binding energy per nucleon of 4He (mass = 4.0026 u). Given m_p = 1.0078 u, m_n = 1.0087 u.

Δm = 2×1.0078 + 2×1.0087 − 4.0026 = 0.0304 u
BE = 0.0304 × 931.5 ≈ 28.3 MeV
BE per nucleon = 28.3 / 4 = 7.075 MeV

Exam tip: Always remember 931.5 conversion factor. Graph questions on BE curve appear every alternate year.

PYQ Type 2: Radioactivity & Half-Life (Numerical + Graph)

Example (JEE Main & HSC 2025 pattern): Activity of a radioactive sample is 200 Bq. After 10 days it is 25 Bq. Find half-life.

\[ \frac{A}{A_0} = \frac{1}{8} = \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^3 \implies n=3 \]
\[ T_{1/2} = \frac{10}{3} \approx 3.33 \text{ days} \]

Exam tip: Questions on mean life, carbon dating, and activity ratio appear every year in both JEE and Maharashtra HSC.

PYQ Type 3: Nuclear Fission Energy Release

Example (JEE Advanced pattern): Energy released when 1 kg of 235U undergoes fission (200 MeV per fission).

Number of atoms = (1000/235) × 6.023×1023
Total energy = 8.2 × 1013 J ≈ 20,000 MW·h (real power plant numbers!)

Pro Tip for 2026 Exams: Practise 2018–2025 JEE PYQs + Maharashtra HSC last 10 years papers. Binding energy curve + half-life numericals = guaranteed 8–10 marks.
